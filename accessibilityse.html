Disability Discrimination in the Sharing Economy Platforms

User Research

Background


  • What is Sharing Economy?
    • New online platforms which connect individuals who are not familiar with each other to exchange goods and labor, e.g., AirBnb, Uber, Lyft, Rover, etc.
  • Why Sharing Economy is potential for people with disabilities?
    • The work is more flexible on Sharing Economy platforms.
    • People with disabilities encountered discrimination in conventional work environment.
    • It's easier for people with disabilities to find part-time job in relative to people without disabiliti
  • Discrimination in Sharing Economy
    • Gender and racial discrimination against both clients and service providers on sharing economy platforms.

Problems

Whether clients will discriminate service providers with disabilities on sharing economy platforms?

  • Types of disability
    • Visible disabilities: e.g., Wheelchair Users v.s. Non-wheelchair Users
    • Invisible disabilities: Physical Disabilities v.s. Psychiatric Disabilities
  • Types of sharing economy work
    • Mobility requirement: low v.s. high
    • Expertise requirement: low v.s. high
    • Online work v.s. offline work

Initial Study

Aim

Identify people's perceptions on different online working platforms, according to:

  • Expertise Requirement
  • Mobility Requirement

Procedures

  • We chose five sharing economy platforms varied by work mobility and expertise.
  • Within experiment: 19 participants evaluated all five platforms based on mobility and expertise requirement.

Findings

  1. Rover and TaskRabbit having more mobility requirement for service providers relative to other platforms.
  2. No apparent difference on expertise requirement for service providers working on those platforms, so we exclude expertise in following studies.

Primary Study

The effect of Visible Disabilities and work's Mobility and Expertise Requirement on Hiring Preference

Aim

Measure people's perceptions and attitudes toward visible disabled online service providers

  • Hiring Preference
  • Perception of Profile Credibility

Method

  • 2 × 2 × 2 × 2 between-groups experiment
  • 160 participants
  • Independent Variables:
    • Physical Status: Wheelchair user v.s. Non-wheelchair user
    • Platforms: Rover v.s. Fiverr
    • Gender
    • Race
  • Dependent Variables:
    • Hiring Preference

Findings

  • A trending significant effect of Gender on Hiring Preference.
  • Nonsignificant effects of Race, Physical Status, and Platform on Hiring Preference.
  • People would be more likely to hire disabled male service providers than the disabled females.
  • Although no statistic difference, the preference for able-bodied workers is slightly higher than the disabled service providers on both platforms.

Reflections

  • The disability discrimination might exist in the sharing economy platforms.
  • People's decisions to or not to choose the disabled servie providers can be based on either their empathy, sympathy, or concerns for the disabled.
    • Some participants perceived the disabled people or the old requiring more help from others, thus hiring them would be charitable.
    • Others were worried about service providers' physical conditions, e.g., caring a dog might cause extra trouble for people with disabilities.

Design Recommendation

  • It is necessary to label the mobility requirement and essential ability requirement for the task.
  • It would be beneficial for service providers to add features relavent to the themes of the sharing economy on their profiles.
  • When publishing the job description, it's necessary to pay attention to language use according with the ADA.
